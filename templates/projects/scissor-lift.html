Scissor Lift

Project in collaboration with MIKEL'S.

Requirements

Manually operated scissor lift capable of tripling its original height and supporting a load of 155 lb, all within a compact footprint of 8.5" × 11". The lift was commissioned by MIKEL’S during COVID-impacted semester, with strict constraints on power, materials, and cost.

Simulations

Each component was dimensioned based on analytical calculations, from bolt diameters to bar thickness, and validated through finite element analysis (FEA) to ensure structural integrity under load.

Dolore Amet Consequat

As design lead, I translated the customer’s requirements into a functional concept. The lift had to be made primarily of aluminum, avoid welded joints, and maintain a safety factor of 1.5 across all components. We began with conceptual sketches, a morphological matrix, and a QFD analysis to prioritize features like load capacity, manufacturability, and elevation ratio.

The final design used a power screw mechanism for actuation, replacing the initial hydraulic concept. We modeled the full system in SolidWorks, including the scissor arms, base, platform, and actuator supports.

Although the physical prototype wasn’t built due to pandemic constraints, we delivered a complete CAD model, manufacturing drawings, and process plans. These included machining operations like drilling, threading, turning, and milling for components such as the scissor arms, sliders, and actuator mounts.

The project was presented to our professor and the company’s head manager, demonstrating a full engineering workflow from customer briefing to validated digital prototype. This project was a deep dive into mechanical design under pressure, balancing customer needs, spatial limitations, and manual operation requirements.

It taught me how to lead a team through technical uncertainty, validate performance through simulation, and deliver a solution that met real-world constraints.
