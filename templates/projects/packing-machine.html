Early Steps

While working at Toyo Kasei, I co-led the design of a semi-automated packing machine to reduce the physical strain on operators handling 20 kg irrigation tape rolls. The traditional packing process required operators to manually lift the roll multiple times and wrap it with plastic film — a repetitive and physically demanding task.

On the left side of the machine, a plastic film roll holder was positioned so the operator only needed to insert the film into the roll’s central hole and activate the machine. The wrapping process was then handled automatically, rotating the roll via two rubber rollers powered by a servo motor and gear system. The gear ratio was carefully calculated to prevent unsafe rotational speeds while maintaining efficient wrapping.

Although I left the company before the machine was fully completed, the structural design was finalized and near implementation. A second phase of the project was planned to include a scissor lift, enabling operators to transfer the roll from the roller machine to the packing station with minimal effort. Even without the lift, the packing machine alone was projected to reduce physical strain by at least 50%.

This project highlighted my ability to design for operator safety, mechanical efficiency, and collaborative development. It also reinforced the importance of integrating motion control, load dynamics, and ergonomic principles into industrial automation.
